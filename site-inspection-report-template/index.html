Inspection report template

A site inspection report template built around decisions, not decoration.

A strong template helps the reader understand the visit, navigate findings, identify required actions, and distinguish open work from accepted closeout.

The operational problem

Use the downloadable sample to evaluate structure, then define your mandatory capture fields before adopting a report format.

Beautiful formatting cannot rescue incomplete field data. The template needs a reliable hierarchy: report context, summary, issue index, evidence pages, action ownership, status, and sign-off or limitations appropriate to the visit.

Who this is for: Teams designing an inspection standard, comparing report tools, or moving away from manually assembling documents after every site visit.

What a reliable field record needs

Every item should be understandable without relying on the inspector’s memory. Keep the description factual, make the location findable, show enough photographic context, and write the required action as an outcome that can be checked. Responsibility and due dates make the register actionable; status and closeout evidence make it traceable.

CaptureArea, trade, owner, priority, status, date, notes, and photographs stay attached to one item.

03

Issue and close

Test the template with a real project, review readability on screen and paper, and revise before rollout.

Field and report checklist

Adapt the checklist to your role, contract, jurisdiction, records policy, and the technical scope of the visit. SiteReportKit organises information; it does not confer inspection authority or make professional judgments for the user.

  • Cover and report identity
  • Executive summary and counts
  • Indexed findings
  • Evidence and annotations
  • Action ownership and dates
  • Status, closeout, and sign-off context

Report quality checks before issue

  1. Confirm the title, author, date, site, client, scope, and intended recipient.
  2. Check that item references are unique and locations use one consistent hierarchy.
  3. Remove duplicates and split conditions that require different owners or closeout decisions.
  4. Verify captions, annotations, names, dates, status, and required actions against the field record.
  5. Open the exported PDF, XLSX, or CSV, inspect image legibility, page breaks, filters, and counts, then store the issued version under the project’s records process.

Frequently asked questions

Questions about inspection report template

Can I download a sample inspection PDF?

Yes. This page links to a real construction sample generated for the SiteReportKit website.

What belongs on the cover?

Typically project, report type, site, client, inspector or author, inspection date, issue date, and any report identifier appropriate to the process.

Should every report include a signature?

That depends on the report’s purpose and governance. A signature should not imply certification or authority beyond the signer’s actual role.

Can a template cover all inspection types?

A common core is useful, but categories, scope wording, evidence, and approval requirements should be adapted to the specific inspection.
