Terminology guide

Punch list vs snag list: different terms, nearly the same operational problem.

The regional vocabulary changes, but the record still needs a precise location, clear evidence, responsible party, required action, status, and verification.

The operational problem

Choose the label recipients recognise, define the report scope, and retain a consistent issue model beneath the terminology.

“Punch list” is dominant in US construction, while “snag list” and “snagging” are common in the UK, Ireland, Australia, and other markets. Neither term alone defines the author’s authority, contractual effect, or technical scope.

Who this is for: Cross-border project teams, software buyers, owners, contractors, and consultants standardising field-report language across regions.

What a reliable field record needs

Every item should be understandable without relying on the inspector’s memory. Keep the description factual, make the location findable, show enough photographic context, and write the required action as an outcome that can be checked. Responsibility and due dates make the register actionable; status and closeout evidence make it traceable.

Field and report checklist

Adapt the checklist to your role, contract, jurisdiction, records policy, and the technical scope of the visit. SiteReportKit organises information; it does not confer inspection authority or make professional judgments for the user.

  • Recognisable regional term
  • Explicit report scope
  • Stable item reference
  • Location and evidence
  • Action owner and target date
  • Closeout state and proof

Report quality checks before issue

  1. Confirm the title, author, date, site, client, scope, and intended recipient.
  2. Check that item references are unique and locations use one consistent hierarchy.
  3. Remove duplicates and split conditions that require different owners or closeout decisions.
  4. Verify captions, annotations, names, dates, status, and required actions against the field record.
  5. Open the exported PDF, XLSX, or CSV, inspect image legibility, page breaks, filters, and counts, then store the issued version under the project’s records process.

Frequently asked questions

Questions about terminology guide

Is a snag list legally different from a punch list?

The terms themselves do not determine legal effect. Contract terms, appointment, jurisdiction, author, scope, and project procedure matter.

Which term should a global product use?

Use the recipient’s familiar term in the report while keeping the underlying item fields consistent.

Is a defects list always more serious?

Not necessarily. Teams use “defect” differently. Define categories and avoid assuming the label alone establishes technical or contractual significance.

Can one app support both?

Yes. SiteReportKit supports punch, snag, defect, observation, and closeout reporting from the same structured field workflow.
